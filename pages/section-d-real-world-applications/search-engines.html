Search Engines

Prepared jointly by: Nupoor Mahajan & Hansa Gusaiwal

What It Does

Search engines take a user's typed query and instantly retrieve the most relevant documents or web pages from billions of possibilities.

Relevant Week 1 Concepts

Tokenization, stop-word removal, TF-IDF, and n-grams are used to break down both the search query and indexed documents into comparable features.

Why Those Concepts Are Needed

Text must be converted into a consistent, comparable numeric form (like TF-IDF vectors) so that the engine can mathematically measure how relevant a document is to a query.

Expected Benefits

Faster, more relevant results; ability to handle typos, synonyms, and different word forms through stemming and lemmatization.

Real-world example When a student searches "cheap laptops for engineering students", the search engine tokenizes the query, removes stop-words like "for", and ranks pages using TF-IDF-style relevance scoring against millions of indexed pages.
Connection to theory Search engines rely heavily on the Feature Engineering concepts - especially TF-IDF - to rank documents by relevance rather than by exact keyword matching alone.
