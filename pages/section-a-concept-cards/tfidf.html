Feature Engineering

TF-IDF

Combining Term Frequency and Inverse Document Frequency into one score that highlights words that are both frequent locally and rare globally.

feature engineeringvectorizationsearch ranking
Prepared by: Hansa Gusaiwal
1 Definition

TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency) is a numerical statistic that combines how often a word appears in a document (TF) with how rare that word is across the entire corpus (IDF), producing a score that highlights genuinely important words.

2 Purpose

TF-IDF solves the main weakness of plain Bag of Words: it stops common, unhelpful words from dominating the feature space, while giving higher importance to words that are frequent in a specific document but rare elsewhere.

3 Working Principle

For every word in every document, TF-IDF is calculated as:

TF - IDF ( w , d ) = TF ( w , d ) × IDF ( w )

A word gets a high TF-IDF score only if it appears often in a particular document AND is relatively rare across the whole collection.

4 Example
Worked example In a set of movie reviews, the word "the" has high TF but very low IDF (since it appears everywhere), so its TF-IDF score stays low.
The word "cinematography" might have moderate TF but high IDF (appearing in only a few reviews), giving it a high, meaningful TF-IDF score.
5 Advantages
  • Balances local importance (TF) with global rarity (IDF) in a single score.
  • Widely used, well understood, and easy to implement with libraries like scikit-learn.
  • Performs noticeably better than raw Bag of Words counts for search and classification.
6 Limitations
  • Still ignores word order and deeper semantic meaning.
  • Produces sparse, high-dimensional vectors for large vocabularies.
  • Cannot capture that "good" and "excellent" are related in meaning.
7 Applications
  • Search engine relevance ranking.
  • Keyword extraction from academic papers or reviews.
  • Text classification and clustering, such as the Amazon reviews sentiment project.
